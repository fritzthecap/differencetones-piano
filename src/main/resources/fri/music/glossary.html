Glossary

The terms defined here are used by this application and may differ from common definitions.

Term Description
Cent An artificial dimension of pitch (tone height). One octave is divided into 1200 Cent, so one semitone is 100 Cent. Humans can perceive inaccuracies of about 4 Cent. You can't convert Hertz (frequency unit) directly into Cents, because Hertz increase logarithmically and Cents linearly.
Combination Tones The set of difference-tones and sum-tones. Summation-tones result from adding frequencies of two interval tones, but they are barely audible. The only clearly audible combination-tone is the primary difference-tone.
Difference-Tone The tone that sounds with the frequency difference of two distinct tones, i.e. highTone.frequency - lowTone.frequency. Barely audible further difference-tones are the secondary with 2 * lowTone.frequency - highTone.frequency and the tertiary with 3 * lowTone.frequency - 2 * highTone.frequency.
Difference-Tone Inversion A difference-tone can be produced by various intervals, e.g. C4 can be produced by [C5 G5] or [G5 C6] or others, depending on the tuning of the used 12-tone scale. The interval [C5 G5] thus is called a difference-tone inversion, another word for difference-tone interval.
Equal Temperament A 12-tone scale that uses equal distances between semitones (100 cents). The most popular tuning of our times, because it provides the most freedom for modulations into other keys.
Fraction-Based Tunings Tunings whose intervals are calculated using certain rational fractions like 3/2 for fifth, 4/3 for fourth, 6/5 for major third etc. For example A4 with 440 Hertz gives 440 * 3/2 = 660 Hertz for E4, which is the fifth to A4. Equal Temperament is no such tuning, it uses an irrational fraction.
Interval Two distinct tones.
IPN International Pitch Notation, a standard naming scheme for 12-tone scales.
Just Intonation A 12-tone scale that uses different distances between semitones. It is calculated by fractions like 3/2 for fifth, 4/3 for fourth, 5/4 for major third, ... The 5-limit tuning is the most popular, well suited for music where harmony is most important.
Melody A sequence of single-voiced notes.
Note A tone with a certain duration and volume.
Scale A subdivision of an octave into various numbers of tone steps that repeat on every octave. 12-tone scales divide an octave into 12 steps. Traditional "modal" scales divide an octave into 7 steps. Both use equal or various step distances.
Tone A sound at a certain frequency (pitch) that has a name, for example "A4" having 440 Hertz.
Tone-System An ordered collection of tones built according to a tuning. It has a name and could contain more than one scale, like it is in Just Intonation.
Triad Three distinct tones, mostly in major/minor thirds distance.
Tuning A certain intonation or temperament, see Equal Temperament and Just Intonation. Instruments need to be tuned to that before a performance.
Wave Generator Electronic wave generators produce very clean tones with a minimum of overtones. They are particularly well suited for producing difference tones. Popular wave forms are Sine (the cleanest), Triangle, Sawtooth, Square.
