Difference Tones

This application gives insight into what two tones could do when sounding at the same time. Difference-tones are part of combination tones and are also called "Tartini tones". They are the acoustic equivalent of moir√© patterns.

Introduction

What you see in the middle of this user-interface is an HTML-browser for viewing the documentations of this application. On both sides you see buttons that will launch new app windows representing aspects of tunings and difference-tones. On most of these windows you will find one or more "Help" buttons that provide information or user-guides about what you can do with those apps, but you can find these documentations also here below.

Intended audience of this educational project are musicians that want to know more about what's sounding there deep under when they bend two high notes on their electric guitar. I also could imagine that small violin- or flute-ensembles might want to try out performing a melody by playing its difference-tone intervals instead of playing the melody directly. For them, there is the "Difference-Tone Composer" app and the "ABC Export" buttons for printing out interval notes on some ABC-translator on the web. (I am looking forward to hear about the first "Difference-Tone Quartet" of the world !-)

Contained Applications

Left side

  1. See and Hear Difference-Tones Generated by Various Intervals

    Activate "Hold" so that any clicked piano key will create a constant sound. Then click a piano key in some upper octave. You can change any parameter on top while the sound plays. To stop the sound, click "Hold" or one of the two piano keys once again.

  2. Find Out Which Intervals Can Generate a Difference-Tone

    Click a piano key (in some upper octave) and see which difference-tone intervals could generate that tone. Click onto a list item and see which piano keys get activated by it. The difference-tone will have a red border.

  3. Listen to Auto-Composed Difference-Tone Intervals for a Melody

    An algorithm will auto-compose difference-tone intervals for you. Enter a melody and click "Play". Be aware that the auto-compose algorithm is quite naive.

  4. Compose Difference-Tone Intervals for a Melody

    The most important but also the most complex user-interface, it unites all components of this project, except frequency sliders. You can compose difference-tone intervals for a melody here, or you can click "Auto-Compose" and then adjust the intervals.

Right side

  1. See Intervals and Their Difference-Tones on Frequency Sliders

    Sliders give more freedom than a piano keyboard. You get two frequency sliders (4 cent precision) for two tones of an interval and can watch and hear where their difference-tone slides to. Further you see the JustIntonation tuning calculation ratios. Optionally you can also watch the second and third difference-tone, although these hardly can be heard.

  2. Study Chord Beatings in Various Tunings with Frequency Sliders

    Sliders provide the frequencies of four different tones. This is for exploration of beatings in complex contexts.

  3. Check Fraction-Based Tunings for Purity

    Just-intonation is not as pure as its name suggests. For example, in a C-major scale (IONIAN mode), the minor chord on D (d-f-a) does not sound good. Find out which intervals and triads are 'unjust'.

More ....

  1. Hear Interval Beatings in Various Tunings

    Explore 'unjustness' of tunings in context of intervals (two tones).

  2. Hear Triad Beatings in Various Tunings

    Explore 'unjustness' of tunings in context of triads (chords with three tones).

  3. Write Notes on the Piano and Play Back the Melody

    Get familiar with the notes syntax. No difference-tones are involved here.

  4. Configure Pianos (API Showcase)

    You can configure different kinds of pianos with lots of properties. Also a MIDI-piano is available, where you can test the sounds of all instruments supported by the Java synthesizer.

About Me

I have been a musician all my life and discovered difference-tones about 40 years ago. Since then I tried to use them in my music. As I am a software developer too, I started to experiment with sound wave generators last year. This application made me understand that difference-tones are more for electronic music, because instruments played by humans will hardly reach the precision needed to generate well audible difference-tones. Moreover most instruments have so many overtones that the audience would not perceive their generated difference-tones - although they are always present like shadows!
