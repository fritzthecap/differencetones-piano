This documentation refers to Java class DifferenceToneForIntervalPiano.

See and Hear Difference-Tones Generated by Various Intervals

Activate the "Hold" checkbox on top left, so that any clicked piano key will create a constant sound. Then click a piano key in some upper octave, e.g. "C5". The configured "Autoplay Interval" tone, always above the clicked one, will be added automatically, in this case "E5". These two interval tones will be marked by a blue border, the resulting difference-tone to the left will be marked by a red border (when the difference-tone is in the keyboard's range). Although it sounds as if, the difference-tone is not played by the piano, it is generated through the interval tones as soon as they leave the loudspeakers membrane. Only two tones go to the speakers, but three are in the air afterwards.

To stop the sound, click "Hold" or one of the pressed keys once again (not the difference-tone!). You can change any parameter on top, e.g. the tuning, while the sound plays.

Everytime you click a piano key, a small blue rectangle will pop up in the middle of the keyboard. There you can see all tones that are actually played by the piano. This control is directly connected to the sound channel and thus provides certainty about what is sent to the loudspeaker.

Tuning Example

When you change the "Tuning" from EqualTemperament to LIMIT_5_SYMMETRIC JustIntonation, you will see the difference-tone jump from "C#3" to "C3". This is a good proof for the fact that JustIntonation is more harmonious than EqualTemperament.

Deviation Example

Change the "Autoplay Interval" from "Major Third" to "Minor Third", go back to EqualTemperament and watch the difference-tone. When you drag the "Deviation Tolerance" slider to the left, you will see that the difference-tone disappears at 34 %.

Choosing "Fifth" as "Autoplay Interval" will generate the same difference-tone in every tuning. But try this: activate "Hold", choose "Fifth", choose EqualTemperament and click the "C5" piano key. The difference-tone "C4" will appear on the left. Now reduce the "Deviation" slider to 12 %. You will see that the difference-tone disappears. This is because the fifth is not a clean interval in EqualTemperament (the only clean interval in EqualTemperament is the octave).

Most JustIntonation tunings need nearly no deviation tolerance to generate clean difference-tones, most work with even 0 %. But try to choose "Fourth" interval, activate "Hold" and tuning LIMIT_5_SYMMETRIC_1 JustIntonation, then click the "A5" piano key. You will see no difference-tone at the default 76 % deviation (38 Cent). Only when you increase the deviation to 89 % (45 Cent), the difference-tone "D#4" will appear! If you try the same with "F5", the difference-tone will disappear on 0 % deviation only. This shows that F and A are "unjust" tones in JustIntonation. More details about "unjustness" you can find in Check Fraction-Bases Tunings for Purity".

The narrower the interval, the more unpredictable the difference-tone, the more it depends on tuning and deviation tolerance. Mind that the difference-tone is always present, reducing the deviation doesn't remove its sound, only the application doesn't accept it as "just" tone any more.

Same Difference-Tone Despite Parallel Interval Move

Set the deviation to 23 %, choose "Fourth", tuning LIMIT_5_SYMMETRIC_1 JustIntonation and play the "A#5" piano key. The difference-tone "E4" appears. Then stop the sound and click piano key "B5". Again the difference-tone "E4" appears, although the interval made a parallel move one semitone higher! If you go back to "A#5" and then reduce the deviation to 22 %, the "E4" difference tone will disappear, although it will still be present with "B5".
