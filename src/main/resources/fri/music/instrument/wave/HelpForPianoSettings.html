Piano Controls

The controls on top of the user-interface affect the piano on bottom.

On/Off Switch

With this switch you can turn off sound while the piano is playing, be it because you click a piano key or the notes-player is running. It's also an emergency switch when something goes wrong with the computer's sound resources, but hopefully you won't need it ever.

Tunings

There are 8 different (hardcoded but extensible) tunings for 12-tone scales (which I took from Wikipedia). Mind that the application is not equipped for others than 12-tone scales!

The EqualTemperament is a modern neutral tuning that tries to balance all dissonances as good as possible by establishing the same distance (100 Cent) between all semitones. This gives a lot of freedom to modulations and chord progressions. Try out a C-major scale, you will see that all difference-tone interval-lists of EqualTemperament have the same structure.

Most of the other tunings are JustIntonation scales. These have variable distances between their semitones. They focus on the good sound of 7-tone diatonic scales, that means there should be less beatings (vibrations, discords) in a polyphonic performance with JustIntonation than in one with EqualTemperament. The word "limit" refers to prime number limits used in the tuning's frequency calculations. Most of the JustIntonation difference-tone interval-lists have different structures.

The Pythagorean is the oldest known mathematically founded tuning of the Western world. It has been abandoned in the 16th century due to its strong beatings.

The Harmonic Series is a tuning derived from overtone analysis. Mind that some of its tones can not be generated by difference-tone intervals: if you load a C-major scale into the text-area, you will see that its "F" and "A" interval-lists are empty (deviation 76 % or 38 Cent).

Deviation Tolerance

If you calculate difference-tones from the frequencies of two higher notes, you will see that the difference-tone's frequency mostly does not match a scale tone. The deviation tolerance defines how much the difference-tone is allowed to be away from a regular scale tone. If you have a deviation of 100 %, the difference tone is allowed to be exactly in the middle between two semitones, which makes it very ambiguous. Therefore the maximum deviation you can configure here is 90 % (45 Cent). A good average value for all tunings is 76 %, this enables also tritone intervals for JustIntonation, which would be out-of-tune else.

Try out a C-major scale, then reduce the deviation to zero. You will see that the difference-tone intervals lists get empty for EqualTemperament, and will be quite reduced for JustIntonation.

Try more things. For example, put deviation back to 76 %, set JustIntonation, then click "C4" and slowly reduce deviation down to zero. You will see that all the intervals from minor third up to major sixth remain in the difference-tone intervals list of "C4". That means the tuning is very clean, in other words, it generates nearly no beatings when used polyhonically.

Narrowest Interval

The closer the tones of an interval are together, the deeper their difference-tone will become. If you have a major second interval, e.g. "[C6 D6]", the differece-tone will be 3 octaves lower on "C3".

If you reduce deviation, you will see that just fifth and fourth will remain as difference-tone intervals. All others are fuzzy and imprecise. Thus it makes few sense to allow intervals smaller than minor-third.

Try to switch to Limit-5-Symmetric-1 JustIntonation (76 % deviation) and configure major-second as narrowest interval. Click the "C3" piano key. In the interval-list below, you will see that both the intervals "[C6 D6]" and "[D6 E6]" generate "C3", which makes major-second very ambiguous.

Widest Interval

The wider the tones of an interval are apart, the higher their difference-tone will become. If you have an octave, the difference-tone will be identical with the lower tone and thus disappear. For that reason it makes few sense to allow intervals greater than major-sixth.

Wave Form

The piano can be played with different sounds. For hearing difference-tones, the best will be the "Sine" wave form, but "Triangle" is also quite good. The less overtones the sound has (sine-wave is the purest), the better you will hear difference-tones.

Amplitude

The volume control (loudness) is called "Amplitude" because the sound of the piano is created by a wave-generator, not a MIDI-synthesizer. Reason for the wave-generator is, you cannot tune a Java MIDI-synthesizer, its tuning is fixed to equal-temperament, but here different tunings are needed to bring light into the nature of difference-tones.
